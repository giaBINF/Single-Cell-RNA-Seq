## Introduction

Influenza A virus (IAV) is a major respiratory pathogen, responsible for an estimated 290,000 to 650,000 influenza associated respiratory deaths worldwide each year.<sup>1</sup> The nasal mucosa serves as the primary site of exposure to IAV infection in mammals, yet immune responses at this site remain poorly understood.<sup>2</sup> In mice, the nasal mucosa is composed of three functionally distinct regions: the respiratory mucosa (RM), olfactory mucosa (OM), and lateral nasal gland (LNG).<sup>3</sup> Following IAV infection, immune cells within the nasal mucosa detect the virus and initiate an antiviral response through the production of interferons (IFNs), which in turn activate interferon stimulated genes (ISGs).<sup>4,5</sup> This immune response occurs in a coordinated, temporal manner, beginning with the recruitment of neutrophils at 2 days post infection (dpi), followed by monocytes and natural killer (NK) cells at 5 dpi, macrophages and T cells at 8 dpi, and finally the establishment of memory immune cells by 14 dpi that support long term protection.<sup>3</sup>

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) enables gene expression profiling at the individual cell level, overcoming the limitation of bulk RNA-seq, which measures only the average expression signal across an ensemble of cells.<sup>6,7</sup> Seurat and Scanpy are the two most widely used platforms for scRNA-seq data analysis.<sup>8</sup> Seurat was selected for this analysis due to its native compatibility with downstream R-based statistical tools used in this study, including DESeq2 for pseudobulk differential expression<sup>9</sup> and clusterProfiler for functional enrichment analysis.<sup>10</sup> Cell type annotation was performed using SingleR with the ImmGen mouse immune cell reference dataset.<sup>11</sup>

This study reanalyzed a scRNA-seq dataset generated by Kazer et al.,<sup>3</sup> comprising 156,572 single-cell transcriptomes from three nasal tissue regions across five time points (naive, 2, 5, 8, and 14 dpi) during primary IAV H1N1 PR8 infection, with three biological replicates per group.<sup>3</sup> The objective was to characterize the cellular landscape of the nasal mucosa during IAV infection and identify differentially expressed genes and enriched pathways in macrophages at peak viral load compared to baseline.

## Methods

### Computational resources  
Analyses were run on the Digital Research Alliance of Canada’s Nibi cluster (R v4.5.0, 128 GB RAM, 4 CPUs).

### Data source and preprocessing  
A pre-processed Seurat object containing scRNA-seq data from Kazer et al.<sup>3</sup> was provided by the course instructor, consisting of 156,572 single-cell transcriptomes across 25,129 genes from murine nasal mucosa at five time points (naive, 2, 5, 8, 14 dpi) across three tissue regions (RM, OM, LNG), with three biological replicates per group. Approximately 36% of cells lacked confident mouse identity assignments and were retained for clustering but excluded from pseudobulk differential expression analysis.

### Quality control  
Mitochondrial content was calculated using `PercentageFeatureSet` in Seurat v5.4.0<sup>12</sup> with pattern `"^mt-"`. Cells were filtered to retain those with 200–6,000 detected genes and <20% mitochondrial reads (156,545 cells retained). The lower bound removes empty droplets, the upper bound excludes doublets, and the mitochondrial threshold removes dying cells.<sup>13</sup>

### Normalization, scaling, and dimensionality reduction  
Counts were log-normalized using `NormalizeData` (`scale.factor = 10000`, default), which normalizes each cell’s counts by total expression, multiplies by 10,000, and log-transforms the result. The top 2,000 highly variable genes were identified using `FindVariableFeatures` (`method = "vst"`, default), which models the mean–variance relationship to select genes varying beyond noise. Data were scaled using `ScaleData` prior to PCA to prevent highly expressed genes from dominating principal components.<sup>13</sup>  

Twenty-five principal components were retained based on elbow plot inspection.

### Clustering and visualization  
A shared nearest-neighbor graph was constructed using `FindNeighbors` (`dims = 1:25`) and cells were clustered using `FindClusters` (`resolution = 0.3`, default Louvain algorithm), yielding 28 clusters.<sup>12</sup> Resolution 0.3 was selected to capture major cell types without over-partitioning. UMAP was used for visualization due to its ability to preserve global structure relative to t-SNE.<sup>14</sup> No batch correction was applied as cells clustered primarily by cell type rather than sample or condition.

### Marker identification and annotation  
Cluster markers were identified using `FindAllMarkers` (`min.pct = 0.25`, `logfc.threshold = 0.25`, `only.pos = TRUE`), which applies more stringent thresholds than Seurat defaults to reduce low-expression or weakly differential genes.<sup>12</sup>  

Automated annotation was performed using `SingleR`<sup>11</sup> with the ImmGen reference from celldex,<sup>15</sup> which contains microarray profiles of 830 purified mouse immune cell populations.<sup>16</sup> Annotations were refined manually using `FeaturePlot` and marker gene `DotPlot` visualizations to correct misassigned non-immune clusters.

### Differential expression and enrichment  
Pseudobulk differential expression was performed on macrophages (D05 vs naive) by aggregating cells per replicate using `AggregateExpression` and testing with DESeq2,<sup>9</sup> which accounts for within-replicate non-independence that can inflate false discovery rates in single-cell analyses.<sup>17</sup>  

Significance was defined as adjusted p < 0.05 and |log₂FC| > 1. Over-representation analysis (ORA) and gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) were performed using clusterProfiler<sup>10</sup> with GO:BP terms and the `org.Mm.eg.db` annotation database,<sup>14</sup> restricting gene sets to 10–500 genes to exclude poorly defined or overly broad pathways.

SingleR automated annotation identified immune populations including macrophages, neutrophils, B cells, and dendritic cells (Figure 5). Non-immune clusters were labeled as "Stem cells" and "ILC" by SingleR. Manual inspection using a DotPlot of established markers (Figure 6) and FeaturePlots showed that the "Stem cells" cluster expressed Omp (olfactory marker protein) and was relabeled as olfactory sensory neurons, while the "ILC" cluster expressed Cd3d and Nkg7 and was relabeled as T/NK cells. The final annotation comprised 12 cell types: macrophages, neutrophils, T/NK cells, T cells, B cells, dendritic cells, epithelial cells, basal cells, olfactory sensory neurons, sustentacular cells, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells (Figure 2).

Pseudobulk analysis of macrophages (D05 vs Naive) identified 62 significant genes (adjusted p < 0.05, |log₂FC| > 1). Of these, 61 were upregulated and one (Sox18, log₂FC = −1.44) was downregulated. The top upregulated genes were Ifit2 (log₂FC = 2.93), Ifit3 (2.70), Isg15 (2.70), Irf7 (2.60), Oasl1 (2.45), and Ifit1 (2.29).

ORA of the 62 significant DE genes identified 128 enriched GO biological process terms (Figure 10). The top terms were "defense response to virus" (26 genes, adjusted p = 6.92 × 10⁻³¹) and "response to virus" (27 genes, adjusted p = 3.06 × 10⁻³⁰). GSEA identified 390 enriched gene sets, of which 330 were positively enriched and 60 were negatively enriched (Figure 11). The most positively enriched terms were "response to interferon-beta" (NES = +2.28) and "negative regulation of viral process" (NES = +2.18). The most negatively enriched terms were "translation at presynapse" (NES = −2.88) and "translation at synapse" (NES = −2.85).

## Discussion

The differential expression results show that macrophages at D05 shift toward an interferon-mediated antiviral state. Of the 62 significant DE genes, 61 were upregulated, most of which are interferon-stimulated genes. This timing aligns with the stepwise immune response described by Kazer et al., where myeloid cell accumulation precedes adaptive immune involvement.<sup>3</sup> This sustained transcriptional activation is consistent with innate immune memory, where tissue-resident macrophages remain in an activated state following infection.<sup>18</sup>  

Among the top upregulated genes, ISG15 has been shown to function as a critical antiviral molecule against influenza (Figure 7), with ISG15-deficient mice displaying increased susceptibility to IAV infection.<sup>19</sup> Members of the IFIT family — Ifit1, Ifit2, and Ifit3 — were also strongly upregulated, consistent with their established role in restricting viral translation by binding eIF3 and recognizing viral RNA.<sup>20</sup> However, this response is not strictly protective: IAV has been shown to repurpose IFIT2 to promote translation of its own mRNAs, suggesting that the IFIT expression observed here may reflect both host defense and viral exploitation.<sup>21</sup>  

Moreover, this antiviral state is also regulated. Upregulation of Irf7 indicated increased interferon production and amplification of the antiviral response, while Oasl1 is co-expressed (Figure 5) and limits this process by preventing the production of IRF7 protein.<sup>22</sup> Together, this pattern is consistent with a negative feedback mechanism, where activation of the interferon response is coupled with factors that reduce its further amplification.  

Beyond immune signaling, GSEA revealed broader transcriptional changes associated with infection (Figure 11). Pathways related to antiviral defense and interferon responses, such as “response to interferon-beta” and “defense response to virus,” were significantly enriched, consistent with the upregulation of interferon-stimulated genes observed in macrophages (Figure 5). Translation-related pathways were also significantly enriched, indicating that genes involved in protein synthesis are well represented among the differentially expressed genes, although the direction of regulation cannot be determined from this analysis alone. In addition, vascular and endothelial pathways were enriched, including Sox18, a transcription factor involved in maintaining vascular structure. Changes in these pathways suggest that infection alters vascular structure and barrier integrity, which is consistent with evidence that influenza can directly disrupt endothelial cells, increasing vascular permeability and contributing to tissue damage.<sup>23,24</sup>  

The results show that macrophages at D05 adopt an interferon-mediated antiviral state, supported by widespread upregulation of interferon-stimulated genes and enrichment of antiviral pathways. This response is not simply amplified but regulated, as indicated by the co-expression of Irf7 and Oasl1 (Figure 5), consistent with a negative feedback mechanism that controls interferon production. In parallel, expression of chemokines such as Ccl2 and Cxcl10 (Figure 8) indicates active recruitment of additional immune cells, linking macrophage activity to broader immune coordination within the tissue. GSEA results further suggest that these responses are accompanied by changes in cellular processes and tissue structure (Figure 11). One limitation of this analysis is that a substantial proportion of cells lacked identity assignments, which reduced the number of biological replicates available for pseudobulk analysis and may have limited statistical power. Overall, these findings support a model in which macrophages regulate antiviral defense while maintaining control over inflammation and tissue integrity in the nasal mucosa.
